# Natural Capital

Natural capital consists of stocks of renewable and non-renewable natural resources that generate the flows of benefits all life depends on.

## Stocks & Flows
The benefits nature provides—often referred to as environmental assets—are composed of stocks and flows:
- Ecosystem assets (stocks): the foundational natural systems
- Ecosystem services (flows): the benefits derived from these systems

Together, when defined by extent, characteristics, and condition, they form a Natural Asset (real asset).

## Accounting: Price, Cost, & Value 

The current financial system fails to fully value the benefits nature provides.

Today, the economy prices only a fraction of natural capital, primarily in the form of:
- Food
- Raw materials
- Energy

The rest is either undervalued, mispriced, or not priced at all. Real assets, such as land and water rights, capture some of this value, but most ecosystem services remain outside market mechanisms.

### The Cost of Overlooking Natural Capital

Because natural capital is not properly priced, the true cost of its degradation and scarcity is rarely accounted for. Only recently have markets begun pricing in ecological risk.

Environmental and economic accounting methods allow us to better quantify this value. However, as we refine our measurements, the value of nature appears infinite—a phenomenon known as the Richardson Effect. This paradox makes full accounting impractical, if not impossible.

### The RealValue of Natural Capital

To address this challenge, we use a more direct and practical approach:

**Value annual flows in relation to the cost of underlying stocks.**

- Annual flows are expressed as Ecosystem Services Value (ESV) in dollars.
- Stocks are valued based on the market price of the real asset plus project costs.
- Extent & Characteristics describe the land cover (e.g., wetlands, forest) in hectares or acres.
- Condition is the actual ecosystem health at a given time.

### Natural Cap Rate

The relationship between flows and stocks can be expressed as the Natural Capitalization Rate:

<div className="not-prose">
  <div className="bg-[rgba(var(--foreground-rgb),0.03)] p-6 rounded-lg my-8">
    <div className="font-mono text-sm">
      <div className="mb-2">Natural Cap Rate = Flows (ESV) / Stocks (Real Asset Value)</div>
      <div>$1,200,000 / $600,000 = 200%</div>
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

Higher Natural Cap Rates indicate ecosystems that are cost-effective to conserve. Lower Natural Cap Rates suggest land that may be better suited for human use (due to high real estate values) or ideal for restoration (if ecosystem condition is low).
